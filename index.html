You or Them

Kai Kurosawa explores two World War II based stories and the morals they teach about survival.
Image Caption

It’s you or them. Are you going to let them die, ensuring your survival but creating a dark cloud that will hang over you for the rest of your life? Or, are you going to save them, knowing that you’ve extended their life by at least a little, but have placed your own survival in the hands of fate? Times of desperation, such as World War 2, produce many such situations that reveal which choice one would make.

2 brave islanders, an elderly paraplegic and a young woman risk their lives sheltering and feeding a shivering, bony figure. The war had been devastating for them both yet they still found the time and resources to care for this poor, weak child. In a neighbouring house, a large man grins as he dreams of his sweet rewards the Nazi officers would give him. Guernsey Literary and Potato Peel Pie Society (2008) , written by Mary Ann Shaffer and Annie Barrows, is a novel composed entirely of letters set in 1940s Guernsey, a British island in the English Channel. During World War II, the island was occupied by the Nazis and throughout the book we learn of life during and post occupation. The book explores many themes including the dynamic of those that are selfish and those that value others above themselves during desperate times.

Guernsey had a few islanders that valued their comfort and luxury above their fellow people. In August 1946, Juliet, the novel’s protagonist, learns about Eddie Meares.

It seems that Eddie Meares was big and nasty and gave/traded/sold information to the German authorities in exchange for favours. (p215)

Eddie was seen showing off his new silver cigarettes case after Elizabeth and Peter were arrested, unabashedly revealing his guilt. Elizabeth was a kind soul who was loved by (nearly) everyone she met and she and Peter had been hiding and caring for a Polish slave worker. After hearing of Eddie’s new prize, a friend of Elizabeth named Dawsey, to put it bluntly, beat Eddie to a pulp.

One hour through the film, there were a handful of soldiers crammed in a small boat being shot at by the Germans. Alex, played by Harry Styles, and some others wanted Gibson to go overboard to lighten the ship since he was French. Tommy exclaimed that Gibson would be slaughtered if he did, Alex then retorting, Better him than me. Tommy replied that this wasn’t fair with Alex finally stating, Survival’s not fair. This lack of empathy for Gibson from Alex reveals how in survival situations, our moral judgment can be compromised. Alex went as far as preferring a fellow soldier dead for his own sake. Furthermore, the constant ticking sound in the background highlights the urgency of the situation causing Alex and others to irrationally think that getting rid of Gibson would make any significant difference. In addition, the close up shots of Gibson’s innocent face contrasted with the insults spoken by Alex lead the audience to view those that value themselves over others in a negative light.
