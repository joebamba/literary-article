You or Them?

Kai Kurosawa explores what World War II narratives can teach us about survival.

It’s you or them. Are you going to let them die, ensuring your survival but creating a dark cloud that will hang over you for the rest of your life? Or, are you going to save them, knowing that you’ve extended their life by at least a little, but have placed your own survival in the hands of fate? Times of desperation, such as World War II, produce many such situations that reveal which choice one would make.


Two brave islanders, an elderly paraplegic and a young woman risk their lives sheltering and feeding a shivering, bony figure. The German occupation has been devastating for them both yet they still found the time and resources to care for this poor, weak child. In a neighbouring house, a large man grins as he dreams of his sweet rewards the Nazi officers would give him. The Guernsey Literary and Potato Peel Pie Society (2008), written by Mary Ann Shaffer and Annie Barrows, is a novel composed entirely of letters set in 1940s Guernsey, a British island in the English Channel. During World War II, the island was occupied by the Nazis and throughout the book we learn of life during and post occupation. The book explores many themes including the dynamic of those that are selfish and those that value others above themselves during desperate times.

Guernsey had a few islanders that valued their comfort and luxury above their fellow people. In August 1946, Juliet, the novel’s protagonist, learns about Eddie Meares.

It seems that Eddie Meares was big and nasty and gave/traded/sold information to the German authorities in exchange for favours.

Eddie was seen showing off his new silver cigarettes case after Elizabeth and Peter were arrested, unabashedly revealing his guilt. Elizabeth was a kind soul who was loved by (nearly) everyone she met and she and Peter had been hiding and caring for the Polish slave worker. After hearing of Eddie’s new prize, a friend of Elizabeth named Dawsey, to put it bluntly, beat Eddie to a pulp.

The childlike reference to Eddie as big and nasty portrays him like a school bully. This, along with the knowledge his snitching has caused death and devastation, compels us to immediately hate him. Eddie’s willingness to betray his fellow islanders, specifically a well beloved one, to the enemy for a mere luxury, not a necessity, highlights his pure selfishness. You should know that Dawsey was typically a reserved man not prone to violence and the reader, at this point of the novel, has grown to love him. The fact that it was Dawsey who beat Eddie further ruins Eddie’s image and makes this punishment feel deserved. All of this conveys to us that we should look down upon those who consider their own pleasure over the wellbeing of their peers.

Contrary to scum like Eddie Meares, there were also islanders like Elizabeth that would go as far as laying their life down for others. Elizabeth was sent to Ravensbrück Concentration Camp, as a result of Eddie’s snitching, and would often help her fellow prisoners. One of them recounted,

Our friend Alina stole a potato but dropped it on the ground. All digging stopped until the overseer could discover the thief. Alina had ulcerated corneas, and it was necessary that the overseers not notice this, for they might think her to be going blind. Elizabeth said quickly she had taken the potato, and was sent to the punishment bunker for one week.

Elizabeth was quick to take the blame, ensuring Alina would live another day, revealing her Christlike selflessness. In her cell, Elizabeth was resilient despite the torture she suffered, whereas another girl who was punished died. This contrast highlights Elizabeth’s strength and perseverance.

On another day, a girl was being beaten senseless by Binta, a Nazi officer.

Elizabeth broke out of our line fast - so fast. She grabbed the rod from Binta's hand and turned it on her, hitting her over and over again. Guards came running and two of them struck Elizabeth to the ground with their rifles. They threw her into a truck and took her again to the punishment bunker.

Elizabeth bolting to stop the beating so fast unveils her inherent nature of helping others, despite the assured physical punishment. All of these positive attributes of Elizabeth convey that valuing others over yourself is admirable.


A few years earlier on the coast of France not far from Guernsey, 400,000 British soldiers waited to be evacuated. The water around the hallowed soldiers' feet was quickly rising in the dark cramped ship. Bullet holes multiplying like rabbits, eyes darting, spontaneous shouts. They were practically choking on the heavy air. Everybody knew it, but who would say it? They were sinking fast and needed to lose weight. Dunkirk (2017), directed by Christopher Nolan, reveals how humanity responds when their survival is at stake, reflecting the historic evacuation.

One hour into the film, there were a handful of soldiers crammed in a small boat being shot at by the Germans. Since Gibson was a French imposter in a British uniform (The French were only meant to evacuate after the British), Alex, played by Harry Styles, and some other soldiers wanted him to go overboard to lighten the ship. Tommy, played by Fionn Whitehead, exclaimed that Gibson would be slaughtered if he did, Alex then retorting, Better him than me. Tommy replied that this wasn’t fair with Alex finally stating, Survival’s not fair. This lack of empathy for Gibson from Alex reveals how in survival situations, our moral judgment can be compromised. Alex went as far as preferring a fellow soldier dead for his own sake. The constant ticking sound in the background highlights the urgency of the situation causing Alex and others to irrationally think that getting rid of Gibson would make any significant difference to their own survival. The close-up shots of Gibson’s innocent face contrasted with the insults spoken by Alex teaches us to view selfish people negatively.

Of course, not everyone on that ship was like Alex. The whole time Alex interrogated Gibson, Tommy defended him. He told Alex, He’s just looking for a way off the sand like the rest of us. Despite the chaotic tension, Tommy spoke calmly and with a level head, in contrast to the brash voices of Alex and others. He realised Gibson was not any different from the rest of them and so was deserving of life just as much. Throughout Dunkirk, the stories jump around to different soldiers, making it difficult to keep track of an individual. This shows how, despite them facing different challenges, they’re all going through a tribulation together, which reinforces Tommy’s view. Tommy was a light of reason in a darkness of madness.


We all say that we would always adhere to our moral compass no matter the situation; however, as has been explored in The Guernsey Literary and Potato Peel Pie Society and Dunkirk, we can never be certain until we hear death knock on the door. We will hopefully never have to go through the same hardships the characters of these stories endured but will still face challenges that require difficult decisions. So who will you choose: you or them?
